Australia's Bushfire Problem

Deepdive into fires in Australia

Fire isn’t just a seasonal challenge for Australia, it’s become part of it's identity. Bushfires have become a defining feature of the country’s environment, occurring more often and leaving a bigger mark than any other natural disaster. This visualisation aims to explore how widespread, intense, and consistent these fires have become. From the sheer number of incidents to the massive areas burned.

First let's take a look at other natural disasters in Australia to put bushfires into context.

The first two maps make the difference impossible to miss. When you compare bushfires with floods and other natural disasters, the dominance of fire jumps out, especially across the southeast, where red dots cluster thick across the map. Floods and other events show up here and there, but bushfires cover the landscape. To further drive the point home, the other natural disaster events are over a 25 year period, whereas the fires shown all occurred within 2019.

It’s a clear visual message, bushfires aren’t just more frequent, they’re more widespread.

Burn Severity and Regional Breakdown

Area burnt % of state

This section highlights both the scale and proportional impact of bushfires across Australia’s states. The donut chart shows the total hectares of land that have experienced fire historically, with the Northern Territory, Western Australia, and Queensland contributing the largest slices, unsurprising given their overall landmass and forest coverage. But the horizontal bar chart reframes this data by showing the percentage of each state’s total land area that has burned. Here, the Northern Territory, South Australia, and Western Australia emerge as the most affected. This shift from absolute to relative impact reveals that although states may have a large difference in hectares burnt, all states still have had more than 50% of the total forests burned at some point.

Final remarks

This visualisation makes it clear that bushfires are Australia’s most frequent and most widespread natural disaster. They outnumber floods, storms, and everything else, and they cause more damage each year. With hotter temperatures and longer fire seasons becoming the new normal, the need for serious action around prevention and adaptation is more urgent than ever.
